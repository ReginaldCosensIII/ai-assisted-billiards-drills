# 1. Purpose of This Document

This brief establishes the baseline direction for the AI Assisted Billiards Drill Projection Software project. It is intended to serve as the central reference point for product strategy, feature prioritization, research, architecture planning, implementation coordination, and future go-to-market work.

The document is not meant to lock every future decision. It is a working brief that should be updated as new research, prototype findings, user feedback, technical constraints, and business opportunities emerge.

# 2. Project Vision

Create the leading software platform for billiards skill development by combining projection-based drill practice, structured instructional content, and AI-assisted coaching into a modern product experience.

The near-term goal is to build a strong, commercially viable training platform that can compete with existing projection drill products. The long-term goal is to expand that platform into a broader billiards improvement ecosystem that includes remote lessons, instructor tools, analytics, premium content, and eventually computer-vision-assisted coaching.

# 3. Mission, Problem, and Opportunity

## Mission

Make high-quality billiards training more repeatable, more intelligent, more accessible, and more engaging through software, projection, instructional media, and AI-assisted guidance.

## Problem Statement

Existing projection-based billiards training products demonstrate that there is real demand for visual drill guidance, repeatable table setup, and structured practice. However, the category still appears constrained by hardware sensitivity, limited software polish, weak content ecosystems, and little evidence of modern AI-driven coaching workflows.

## Market Opportunity

The project can pursue a differentiated position by combining a polished product experience, better instructional design, recurring product improvement through subscriptions, and a credible path toward intelligent drill coaching. The opportunity is not only to match current market offerings, but to build the category's modern standard.

# 4. Product Thesis

The product should not be framed as only a projector tool or only an AI coach. Its strongest position is as a complete training platform for serious billiards players.

**•** Win first on the fundamentals: projection workflow, calibration, drill clarity, instructional content, and practice usability.

**•** Use AI in version one as a drill-aware coaching assistant rather than a fully autonomous shot-analysis system.

**•** Create a product model that rewards continuous innovation through subscription revenue rather than one-time software sales alone.

**•** Design the platform to expand naturally into instructor services, remote lessons, premium content, and later computer vision.

# 5. Strategic Product Positioning

Recommended positioning statement: a modern projection training platform for serious billiards players.

This positioning is stronger for early go-to-market than claiming a fully autonomous AI billiards coach. It provides a credible first wedge into the market while leaving room to layer in more advanced intelligence over time.

## Differentiators

**•** Better user experience and setup flow than legacy-looking niche competitors.

**•** Drill-aware AI assistance tied to instructional context and player feedback.

**•** A richer content ecosystem built around drills, progressions, lessons, and media.

**•** A more modern business model with recurring updates, data, and premium features.

**•** A clear long-term path toward instructor tools, remote coaching, and marketplace features.

# 6. Core User Segments

**Serious home players:** Primary MVP segment. These users already invest in cues, lessons, table time, and training tools, and are likely to value repeatable structured practice.

**Instructors and coaches:** High-value strategic segment. They can become both customers and content partners by using the platform for lesson delivery, student assignments, and packaged drill programs.

**Pool halls, training rooms, and academies:** Promising later-stage business segment. These users may want premium installations, multi-user access, challenge workflows, and coaching packages.

**Entertainment venues:** Possible long-term expansion segment, but not recommended as the first wedge because it changes the product from serious training toward venue operations and gamification too early.

# 7. Product Principles

**•** Build something genuinely useful before building something fully automated.

**•** Avoid overcommitting to camera-based intelligence in the first version.

**•** Keep the first release focused on a repeatable and satisfying practice loop.

**•** Design every future feature so it strengthens the training value, not just the novelty.

**•** Use software and content quality as the first moat, then data and intelligence as later moats.

# 8. Version 1 MVP Definition

Version one should focus on delivering a premium projection drill platform with strong instructional support and AI coaching based on user-reported outcomes. The system does not need to automatically observe shots in the first release.

## Version 1 Product Goal

Help players understand, execute, repeat, and improve billiards drills through clear projection, strong guidance, and contextual AI support.

## In Scope

**•** Table calibration and projection alignment.

**•** Support for common table sizes and common drill presentation formats.

**•** Drill library with categories, filters, skill tags, and saved favorites.

**•** Drill detail pages with setup, objective, difficulty, and execution guidance.

**•** Instructional media including text guidance and demo videos where available.

**•** An AI assistant tied to the current drill and its knowledge context.

**•** A structured player feedback flow after each drill attempt.

**•** Practice session history and baseline progress tracking.

**•** Possibly guided practice playlists or training tracks if they fit the initial schedule.

## Out of Scope for Initial MVP

**•** Automatic camera-based shot detection.

**•** Real-time cue-ball and object-ball tracking.

**•** Automatic spin, speed, or stroke diagnosis from video.

**•** Full marketplace and instructor management tooling.

**•** Broad entertainment or venue-focused game modes.

# 9. Core MVP Practice Loop

**1.** The player selects a drill.

**2.** The software projects the drill layout and explains the setup and objective.

**3.** The player reviews text guidance, success criteria, and optional demo media.

**4.** The player attempts the drill.

**5.** The player reports the outcome through simple feedback inputs or natural language.

**6.** The AI interprets the result using drill context and provides next-step coaching.

**7.** The player repeats the attempt and can log progress over time.

# 10. AI Role in Version 1

The version one AI should act as a drill-aware coach, not as a computer-vision referee. Its value comes from understanding the selected drill, the desired result, common failure modes, and the player's reported outcome.

## What the AI Should Know

**•** The drill objective and intended skill development.

**•** The expected ball paths or result criteria at a practical coaching level.

**•** Common misses and likely causes.

**•** Corrective advice that can be tried on the next attempt.

**•** Possible alternative routes or acceptable variations when relevant.

## What the Player Can Report

**•** Whether the object ball was pocketed.

**•** Whether the cue ball landed short, long, left, or right of the target area.

**•** Whether the shot felt overcut or undercut.

**•** Whether the player used spin and whether it seemed to hurt the line.

**•** Whether speed felt too soft or too firm.

**•** Whether a scratch or major error occurred.

This model creates a valuable intelligence layer without requiring advanced computer vision in the first release.

# 12. Business Model Direction

The project should be designed as a subscription-first product with optional service and marketplace revenue layered in over time.

## Primary Revenue Lanes

**•** Consumer subscription tiers for drill access, AI guidance, analytics, and premium content.

**•** Higher-tier accounts for serious users, instructors, and performance-oriented players.

**•** Commission or platform fees on remote lessons and instructor bookings in later phases.

**•** Premium drill packs, lesson bundles, or branded training programs.

**•** Potential venue or academy licensing once the product matures.

## Marketplace Concept

A later-stage version of the platform can support a two-sided ecosystem. Players use the software for training and remote coaching, while instructors and top-level players use the platform to offer lessons, packaged expertise, and customer-facing services.

This model can create a flywheel: better instructors produce better content, better content attracts more players, more players increase the value of the platform, and growing activity improves the quality of the coaching layer and business data.

# 13. Technical Direction

The first architecture should prioritize practicality and control rather than trying to force every feature through a phone-only experience.

**•** A local compute layer may be appropriate for projection control and stable on-table use.

**•** A mobile companion experience can be used for control, input, audio, and convenience.

**•** Cloud services can support accounts, subscriptions, analytics, content delivery, and AI features.

**•** The architecture should anticipate future expansion into instructor tools and richer media workflows.

**•** The system should be designed so advanced shot analysis can be added later without rewriting the product foundation.

# 14. Prototype and Hardware Strategy

A high-end projector is not required to begin software prototyping. Early prototype validation and production-like brightness validation are separate stages.

**•** Early development can use a cheaper or older projector in a controlled darker environment.

**•** Early tests should focus on geometry, calibration, UI flow, drill visibility, and basic projection logic.

**•** Later validation should include brighter projectors and real table-light conditions.

**•** Hardware testing should eventually cover multiple projector classes, price points, room conditions, and table setups.

This staged approach reduces the barrier to entry while still preserving a path to real-world hardware validation.

# 15. Major Risks and Constraints

|  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Risk** | **Description** | **Early Mitigation** |
| Overbuilding AI | Trying to solve full automatic shot understanding before shipping a useful product. | Keep version one centered on drill-aware guidance and manual player feedback. |
| Hardware friction | Projection brightness, mounting, and room conditions can complicate adoption. | Prototype cheaply first, then validate with more realistic hardware setups. |
| Content depth | Weak drill content would limit coaching quality and user retention. | Treat content design as a core product asset, not an afterthought. |
| Data scarcity | There may be limited structured data for advanced coaching models early on. | Use expert-authored drill context and player-reported outcomes first. |
| Scope creep | The vision spans software, AI, hardware, media, and marketplace features. | Maintain clear phases, explicit non-goals, and disciplined MVP boundaries. |

**•** What is the minimum acceptable prototype hardware configuration for software validation?

**•** What drill taxonomy and metadata model will best support both product UX and AI context grounding?

**•** How much content is required for a credible beta or early paid release?

**•** What user feedback flow is simplest while still giving the AI enough useful context?

# 19. Baseline Conclusion

The clearest first version of this project is a projection-based billiards training platform with drill-specific AI coaching, instructional content, and player-reported outcome feedback.

That scope is practical, differentiated, and commercially meaningful. It creates a path to launch without waiting on advanced computer vision, while preserving a strong long-term expansion path into instructor services, remote lessons, and progressively more intelligent coaching capabilities.
